# THE LAST EMBER
### An original adventure path for D&D 5e (2014), levels 1–20

> A fallen guardian is teaching the world that death can be hoarded. The party
> spends twenty levels and four planes proving him wrong — or, by accident,
> proving him right.

**Companion files**
- `Appendix-A-Halthurax.md` — the recurring villain's four-form stat progression
- `Appendix-B-Legacy-Items.md` — the bonded "ember-forged" item subsystem

---

## 1. The one idea everything hangs on: the Ember

Every living soul carries a mote of a single, finite primordial fire — the **Ember**.
You die, your mote returns to **the Source** to be rekindled into new life. That is
the natural order, and it has always been tended by an order of guardians called the
**Emberwardens**.

**Undeath is theft.** A soul that refuses to return — a soul *hoarded* — is one the
world never gets back. The supply is finite. Every skeleton, ghoul, wraith, and lich
is a withdrawal that is never repaid.

From this one rule, the whole campaign falls out.

Undead matter, because every undead you fight is a stolen ember. When you destroy one, you
either return its mote (the merciful but harder path) or scatter it (faster, but the mote is
lost to the Source forever). That choice is a recurring micro-decision that feeds Conflict #2.

Death has weight, because the party's own motes are in play. As the Ember drains, dying gets
costlier and stranger (see §7).

And the villain isn't evil for fun. Halthurax believes he is saving souls from the waste of
returning, which makes him the most dangerous kind of antagonist: a protector who has decided
that the thing he protects is the problem.

### The world in one paragraph

The realm of **Aurevel** is a temperate, lived-in heroic-fantasy world of walled towns, old
kingdoms, and trade roads. For three generations now, though, something has been off. The dead
don't always rest, and harvests of newborn life run thin: fewer children, weaker springs,
guttering hearths. People call it the **Dimming**, and they blame everything but the truth. The
truth is a gold dragon a thousand miles away, quietly siphoning the world's soul-fire into
himself.

---

## 2. The villain: Halthurax, the Goldwyrm Who Would Not Burn

Halthurax was once an **ancient gold dragon** and the appointed Warden-Drake of the Ember, the
single greatest guardian the order ever had. Over the centuries he watched countless souls
"waste" themselves returning to the Source, and his grief slowly hardened into conviction:
death is the enemy, and a guardian who lets his charges die has failed them. So he began the
long ritual of lichdom, not for power, but to master death so completely that no soul under his
care would ever have to return.

He does not hate the party. He pities them. To him, they are children defending a house fire
because it's the only home they've ever known.

### Why he grows with you

His phylactery is not a single object. He has distributed it across the embers he has hoarded:
thousands of stolen motes spread across the planes. You cannot destroy him until you have unmade
his hoard, plane by plane. Every time you "kill" a form, you free part of the hoard, which is
good for the world, but the surviving remnant of him reconstitutes around what's left, more
concentrated and more complete each time. Your victories are his molting. Full progression and
stat blocks live in `Appendix-A-Halthurax.md`.

| Tier | Levels | Plane | Halthurax's form | One-line of what he's doing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | 1–4 | **Material Plane** — the Marrowmoor | **The Herald & the living Wyrm** | Anchoring the ritual in the borderlands; the dead can't rest. |
| II | 5–10 | **The Feywild** — the Verdant Pyre | **The Dragon-Wraith** | Blighting the wellspring of living vitality to fuel the ascent. |
| III | 11–16 | **The Shadowfell** — the Hoard-Kingdom | **The Dracolich** | Reigning over a kingdom of hoarded souls; building his death-court. |
| IV | 17–20 | **The Emberthrone** — a plane he is *building* at the Source | **The Becoming God** | Replacing the Source itself; becoming the thing all souls must pass through. |

---

## 3. Structure at a glance

There are four tiers, four planes, and four forms. Each tier is its own mini-arc, and they all
share the same shape: the party arrives, learns what Halthurax is doing there, confronts the
local face of his work, "defeats" him and frees part of the hoard, and then discovers that the
victory only made him stronger. The three non-combat conflicts (§5) run underneath all four
tiers the whole time.

| Tier | Levels | Plane | Halthurax's form | The hook |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | 1–4 | Material | Marrowmoor | Herald / living Wyrm — "the dead won't rest" |
| II | 5–10 | Feywild | Verdant Pyre | Dragon-Wraith — "the wellspring is blighting" |
| III | 11–16 | Shadowfell | Hoard-Kingdom | Dracolich — "he has built a kingdom" |
| IV | 17–20 | Emberthrone | the Source | Becoming God — "he is replacing death itself" |

## 4. The four tiers in detail

Each tier below gives you the plane and gateway, the spine of play, two or three key
set-pieces, the tier boss beat, and the growth trigger (what advances the villain, the items,
and the clocks).

### TIER I — THE MARROWMOOR (Material Plane), levels 1–4

**Gateway:** None. This is home — a fog-bound borderland of moors and barrow-mounds, with a
single struggling town, **Lantern's Rest**, whose dead have begun walking back to their own
doorsteps.

**Spine:** Start small and human. The party are locals or newcomers hired to deal with
"grave-trouble," and they slowly uncover that the restlessness radiates from an old
barrow-complex called the **Marrowmoor Vault**. Down there, a robed figure known as the
**Herald** — Halthurax's mortal agent, a death-priest carrying a shard of his will — is
performing the anchoring rite that lets the Dimming reach this region.

**Key set-pieces**
1. **The doorstep dead** (lvl 1). A returned villager won't stop coming home. This is the
   first undead fight and the first chance to return or scatter a mote, which introduces the
   moral micro-choice that runs through the whole campaign.
2. **The Vault descent** (lvl 2–3). A classic barrow dungeon of traps, ghoul packs, and a
   guardian **wight**, all of it building toward the Herald's ritual chamber.
3. **The Bonding** (lvl 1, the session-zero payoff). At a barrow-altar, each PC's chosen item
   drinks a mote of their own fire and wakes. The legacy items come online here.

**Tier boss beat:** The party faces the Herald, and behind and above the Vault they glimpse
**Halthurax himself** — alive, vast, and golden — for one terrifying, un-fightable moment. He
speaks to them kindly. He is not the cackling-villain type; he simply warns them to stop, for
their own sake, and leaves. He is CR ~17 here, so play this as a scene, not a fight.

**Growth trigger:** Destroying the Herald frees the local hoard, which is good, but it also
completes the anchor, which is bad: Halthurax no longer needs the Material Plane and withdraws
fully into the planes, beginning his molt to Wraith. The first legacy-item milestone fires at
the start of Tier II, and the conflict clocks all set their opening positions here.

**The revenant ally enters (Conflict #3):** In the Vault, the party can free **Sera Vant**, a
knight whose mote was half-claimed. As a **revenant**, she should be hostile, but instead she
latches onto the party as her only tether to staying herself, and she joins them. The thread
back to Halthurax is already in her chest.

---

### TIER II — THE VERDANT PYRE (Feywild), levels 5–10

**Gateway:** A fey crossing in the deep Marrowmoor woods, opened by following Sera's pull or by
an Emberwarden guide. In the Feywild, soul-fire blooms visibly — this is a realm of living
light called the **Verdant Pyre** — and it is going gray at the edges. Halthurax is blighting
the wellspring of vitality to feed his ascent.

**Spine:** This is the tier where the **Emberwardens (Conflict #1)** become central. Their
fractured fey-court chapter, the **Court of First Light**, is split between the **Keepers**, who
return the dead the old way, and the **Tallymasters**, who hoard against the Dimming using
Halthurax's logic dressed up as mercy. The party's deeds across the tier tip the court one way
or the other, and the court's allegiance decides who fights alongside them at the finale.

**Key set-pieces**
1. **The graying glades** (lvl 5–6). Fey of light and growth have been twisted into
   wraith-things, and the party learns that the blight spreads from a wound in the world.
2. **The Tallymasters' bargain** (lvl 7–8, mostly social). A powerful archfey offers the party
   a shortcut: accept hoarded fey-light and power up faster. Taking it is real power, and real
   corruption in Conflict #1. Refusing is harder and slower. No combat required.
3. **The Dragon-Wraith's hunt** (lvl 9). Halthurax's second form stalks the party through the
   dimming Feywild, a chase-and-skirmish set-piece where he can't yet be killed, only driven
   off.

**Tier boss beat:** At the wound in the world, the **Pyre's Heart**, the party confronts the
**Dragon-Wraith** (CR ~13). Defeating it heals the Feywild — a huge, visible good — and frees a
second swathe of hoard. But the freed motes pour toward the Source, and Halthurax rides them
down into the **Shadowfell**, completing his lichdom. That is his second molt.

**Growth trigger:** The second legacy-item milestone fires. The Court of First Light's final
lean, Keeper or Tallymaster, is locked here, so record it. Meanwhile Sera's thread tightens as
she starts losing memories to Halthurax, and Conflict #3 escalates.

---

### TIER III — THE HOARD-KINGDOM (Shadowfell), levels 11–16

**Gateway:** A soul-fall in the Shadowfell, where you follow the freed motes down. The
Shadowfell here is no longer a wasteland: Halthurax has built a kingdom out of hoarded souls,
the gray city of **Ossuara**, where the comfortable dead serve a king who promises they'll
never have to return.

**Spine:** This is the horror tier, and the most morally loaded one. Ossuara is not a
torment-pit; it is pleasant, and that is exactly the point. Its citizens are happy hoarded souls
who do not want to be "freed," and the party has to decide what they are actually here to do.
Will they liberate people who'd rather stay dead? This is where Halthurax's thesis is most
seductive, and where a corrupted Emberwarden party may simply agree with him.

**Key set-pieces**
1. **The grateful dead** (lvl 11–12). Social play and exploration through Ossuara, where the
   party meets souls they personally "returned" earlier who are now angry at them for it.
2. **The death-court** (lvl 13–14). Politics among the dracolich's lieutenants — **bone-knight
   generals**, an **undead red dragon vassal**, and a chancellor who turns out to be Sera's
   original liege. Conflict #3 can resolve here, as Sera confronts the lord who damned her.
3. **The siege of the Pale Hoard** (lvl 15). A full undead-army set-piece assaulting the vault
   where Halthurax keeps the densest part of his phylactery.

**Tier boss beat:** **Halthurax the Dracolich** (CR ~21) waits in his throne-vault. This is the
first time he can be truly hurt, and the first time a PC death is genuinely expected (§7).
Beating him here shatters the kingdom and frees the great mass of the hoard. But the freed
motes have nowhere to return — the Source is failing from the Dimming — so they follow
Halthurax up to the one place still warm: the Source itself. In his final molt, he goes to
become it.

**Growth trigger:** The third legacy-item milestone fires. The Ember Clock (Conflict #2) hits
its crisis threshold, and resurrection now has full teeth (§7). Whether the freed souls have
anywhere to go depends on how full the Source still is, which in turn depends on how the party
played Conflict #2 the whole way down.

---

### TIER IV — THE EMBERTHRONE (the Source), levels 17–20

**Gateway:** Ascend the soul-stream itself, against the current, to the **Emberthrone** — a
half-built plane Halthurax is raising around the dying Source so that he becomes the gate every
soul must pass through. He is no longer hiding his pity. He believes he is one ritual away from
ending death forever, and he means to take the party with him into eternity.

**Spine:** Everything pays off here. The Emberwardens you redeemed or corrupted arrive as your
army or your enemies (Conflict #1). The Ember Clock decides whether the Source can be refilled
or only defended (Conflict #2). Sera's fate as defector, martyr, or freed woman lands here
(Conflict #3). And the legacy items reach their capstone form.

**Key set-pieces**
1. **The Choir of the Hoarded** (lvl 17–18). The assembled stolen souls, sung into a single
   chorused mind, defend the Throne. The party has to unmake the hoard one last time, which
   means convincing or releasing souls who have been dead for a thousand years.
2. **The Two Endings of Death** (lvl 19, the campaign's thesis scene). Halthurax offers a final,
   genuine bargain: help him succeed and no one ever dies again, including the PCs' own dead.
   The party can take it. The campaign supports both answers (§6).
3. **The Becoming God** (lvl 20). The final battle (CR ~26 across phases) at the lip of the
   Source. Halthurax fights with the whole hoard as hit points, and the party wins by emptying
   him, returning the embers, even at the cost of their own.

**Capstone & ending:** §6.

## 5. The three non-combat conflicts (the slow burn)

These run across all 20 levels and resolve through choices, relationships, and resource
management, never through a required fight. Each one is a tracked clock or meter that the GM
nudges at the tier breaks.

### Conflict #1 — Redeem or corrupt the Emberwardens

**Meter:** a scale running from **Keepers (+)** to **Tallymasters (−)**, starting at 0. The
Emberwardens are Halthurax's own old order, now split between returning the dead and hoarding
against the Dimming. The party tips the scale by their deeds. Every time they return a mote
instead of scattering it, honor a Keeper, or refuse a hoarding shortcut, the meter moves toward
the plus. Every shortcut taken, every "merciful" hoard, and every Tallymaster favor moves it
toward the minus.

**Payoff:** at Tier IV the Wardens arrive either as allies (Keeper) or as a second enemy army
that agrees with Halthurax (Tallymaster). Mid-campaign, the meter gates intel, sanctuary, and
resurrection access. Redeeming them repudiates the villain's thesis; corrupting them proves it.

### Conflict #2 — The dwindling resource: the Ember itself

**Meter:** the **Ember Clock**, a pool representing the world's remaining soul-fire that only
ever decreases. It starts at 12 segments. It drops 1 automatically at each tier break (the
Dimming at work), plus 1 each time the party scatters a mote for speed instead of returning it.
Nothing adds to it — you can only slow it, never refill it until the very end.

The effects shift by threshold:
- 12–9: the world is merely "off," and resurrection magic works normally.
- 8–5: the Dimming is visible. Raise Dead and Revivify cost **1 extra Ember segment** — a real,
  shared, dwindling resource — on top of their usual components.
- 4–2 (locked in by Tier III): resurrection requires spending another living being's mote, or
  it returns the PC **Touched** (§7).
- 1–0: no natural resurrection remains, and the only way back is at the Source in Tier IV.

This is the spine of the player-death stakes (§7).

### Conflict #3 — The fraying loyalty: Sera Vant, the revenant ally

**Meter:** the **Tether**, a measure of how much of Sera is still hers rather than Halthurax's,
running from 10 (whole) down to 0 (his). It starts at 7, since she is already half-claimed when
you meet her. She loses a point at each tier break as his ascent pulls at her, and she can only
gain points through the party's investment: keeping promises to her, helping her confront her
past (the chancellor in Tier III), and choosing the merciful-but-costly options she cares about.

Her resolution is decided by the Tether and the party's choices, with no boss fight required:
- **Tether 7+ at the finale:** Sera is freed. She returns her own mote willingly and dies at
  peace, or survives reincarnated — the campaign's gentlest grace note.
- **Tether 3–6:** Sera martyrs herself, spending her remaining self to open the path to the
  Source. She is the party's costliest ally.
- **Tether 0–2:** Sera defects. Halthurax claims her, and she stands among his lieutenants at
  the Emberthrone — the party's failure with one person, made flesh.

## 6. Endings (player death is expected; so is moral weight)

The thesis scene (Tier IV, §4) is real: the party can side with Halthurax, and the campaign
supports it. Track which of three endings the party earns.

**The Old Order Restored** (refuse him, refill the Source) is achievable only with a healthy
Ember Clock and redeemed Wardens. The party returns the hoard, the Source rekindles, and death
resumes its rightful, sad, necessary work. Their own dead stay dead, and that is the price of a
living world. This is the hardest and truest heroic-dark-fantasy ending.

**The Pyrrhic Throne** (defeat him with a gutted Ember Clock) is a win, but the Source is too
drained to refill. The world survives, diminished — fewer souls, a long winter of scarcity. It
is a victory that costs the future.

**The Mercy of Never** (join him) means no one ever dies again, including everyone the party
loved. But the world slowly stops being born: a paradise with no children, ruled by a grieving
god and his hero-lieutenants. This is the seductive ending, played straight and not punished
with a cheap "gotcha."

**Capstone for survivors:** each surviving PC's legacy item reaches its final form (Appendix
B). PCs who died and were returned at the Source in Tier IV come back whole — it is the one
place the Dimming can be undone — which closes the death arc on a note of grace.

## 7. Death with teeth (the player-death subsystem)

Player death is expected, and it means something, because it is wired to Conflict #2.

When a PC dies, their mote tries to return to the Source, but Halthurax's drain contests it.
Resurrection is governed by the Ember Clock thresholds in §5.

A PC raised while the Ember is low — that is, returning at Clock 4–2 — comes back changed, or
Shadowfell-marked. Roll or choose from the **Touched** table: a cosmetic mark (ash-gray veins,
a cold shadow), a new flaw (a hunger, a fear of warmth, a name they answer to that isn't
theirs), and a small edge (resistance to necrotic, advantage on death saves, or a
once-per-long-rest minor necrotic strike). Touched is a story state, not a penalty spiral.

Permanent loss is on the table at Clock 1–0 until Tier IV, and that is by design. A PC who dies
in Tier III may simply be gone until the Source is reached, where a single great act of return
can restore the fallen. That makes the climb to the Emberthrone personal.

Return versus Scatter, the recurring undead micro-choice, is how players feel their hand on the
Clock all campaign long, well before anyone actually dies.

## 8. Session-zero kit

- **Pitch in one line:** "A fallen guardian is hoarding the world's souls to end death forever,
  and he might be right. Stop him before the world runs out of life to be born."
- **Bonded items:** each player picks an object meaningful to their PC — a weapon, a holy
  symbol, an heirloom, an instrument — and it becomes their ember-forged legacy item at the
  Tier I barrow-altar. Build it together using `Appendix-B-Legacy-Items.md`.
- **Tone dial:** heroic dark fantasy. There is real dread and real loss, but the party are
  heroes, color and warmth survive between the horror, and mercy is always available, just
  costly.
- **The three clocks** (Wardens meter, Ember Clock, Sera's Tether): show players the Ember
  Clock openly so they feel it drain, and keep the Wardens meter and the Tether semi-hidden,
  revealed through the fiction.
- **Safety:** undeath, grief, and "is death good?" are core themes, so line up tools like the
  X-card and lines & veils in session zero.
- **Expectation-setting:** tell players up front that death is part of this story, and coming
  back is never free. That's the game.

## 9. Running notes & dragons-as-a-plus

- **Dragons:** Halthurax is the spine, but seed living dragons throughout as foils — a
  frightened young gold who remembers what Halthurax once was (a Tier I–II ally), the **undead
  red dragon vassal** in Ossuara (Tier III), and the choice in Tier IV of whether to redeem or
  destroy his dragon-lieutenants. Underneath everything, the campaign is an argument among
  dragons about death.
- **Pacing the molts:** never let a tier-boss "kill" feel like a cheat. Always give the party a
  real, visible win for the world — a region healed, a kingdom freed — in the same beat that
  reveals the villain has molted. Hope and dread in the same scene is the register you want.
- **CR scaling:** Halthurax's forms (Appendix A) are tuned to be beatable at the top of each
  tier, not the bottom. The early Material-Plane sighting is deliberately un-fightable, so sell
  it as awe, not a TPK.
