# THE LAST EMBER
### An original adventure path for D&D 5e (2014), levels 1–20

> A fallen guardian is teaching the world that death can be hoarded. The party
> spends twenty levels and four planes proving him wrong — or, by accident,
> proving him right.

**Companion files**
- `Appendix-A-Halthurax.md` — the recurring villain's four-form stat progression
- `Appendix-B-Legacy-Items.md` — the bonded "ember-forged" item subsystem

---

## 1. The one idea everything hangs on: the Ember

Every living soul carries a mote of a single, finite primordial fire — the **Ember**.
You die, your mote returns to **the Source** to be rekindled into new life. That is
the natural order, and it has always been tended by an order of guardians called the
**Emberwardens**.

**Undeath is theft.** A soul that refuses to return — a soul *hoarded* — is one the
world never gets back. The supply is finite. Every skeleton, ghoul, wraith, and lich
is a withdrawal that is never repaid.

From this one rule, the whole campaign falls out:

- **Undead matter.** Every undead you fight is a stolen ember. Destroying one either
  *returns* its mote (you choose the merciful, harder path) or *scatters* it (faster,
  but the mote is lost to the Source forever). This choice is a recurring micro-decision
  that feeds Conflict #2.
- **Death has weight.** The party's own motes are in play. As the Ember drains,
  dying gets costlier and stranger (§7).
- **The villain isn't evil for fun.** Halthurax believes he is *saving* souls from the
  waste of returning. He is the most dangerous kind of antagonist: a protector who
  decided the thing he protects is the problem.

### The world in one paragraph
The realm of **Aurevel** is a temperate, lived-in heroic-fantasy world — walled towns,
old kingdoms, trade roads — but for three generations something has been *off*. The
dead don't always rest. Harvests of newborn life run thin: fewer children, weaker
springs, guttering hearths. People call it **the Dimming** and blame everything but the
truth. The truth is a gold dragon a thousand miles away, quietly siphoning the world's
soul-fire into himself.

---

## 2. The villain: Halthurax, the Goldwyrm Who Would Not Burn

Once an **ancient gold dragon** and the appointed Warden-Drake of the Ember — the single
greatest guardian the order ever had. Over centuries he watched countless souls "waste"
themselves returning to the Source, and grief turned to conviction: **death is the enemy,
and a guardian who lets his charges die has failed them.** So he began the long ritual of
lichdom — not for power, but to *master* death so completely that no soul under his care
need ever return.

He does not hate the party. He pities them. He thinks they're children defending a house
fire because it's the only home they've known.

### Why he grows with you
His phylactery is not one object. He has **distributed it across the embers he has
hoarded** — thousands of stolen motes spread across the planes. You cannot destroy him
until you have unmade his hoard, plane by plane. Every time you "kill" a form, you free
*part* of the hoard — which is good for the world — but the surviving remnant of him
reconstitutes around what's left, more concentrated and more complete. **Your victories
are his molting.** Full progression and stat blocks: `Appendix-A-Halthurax.md`.

| Tier | Levels | Plane | Halthurax's form | One-line of what he's doing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | 1–4 | **Material Plane** — the Marrowmoor | **The Herald & the living Wyrm** | Anchoring the ritual in the borderlands; the dead can't rest. |
| II | 5–10 | **The Feywild** — the Verdant Pyre | **The Dragon-Wraith** | Blighting the wellspring of living vitality to fuel the ascent. |
| III | 11–16 | **The Shadowfell** — the Hoard-Kingdom | **The Dracolich** | Reigning over a kingdom of hoarded souls; building his death-court. |
| IV | 17–20 | **The Emberthrone** — a plane he is *building* at the Source | **The Becoming God** | Replacing the Source itself; becoming the thing all souls must pass through. |

## 3. Structure at a glance

Four tiers, four planes, four forms. Each tier is its own mini-arc with the same shape:
**arrive → learn what he's doing here → confront the local face of his work → "defeat"
him and free part of the hoard → discover it made him stronger.** The three non-combat
conflicts (§5) run *underneath* all four tiers the whole time.

```
T I  (1–4)   MATERIAL    Marrowmoor        Herald / living Wyrm     "the dead won't rest"
T II (5–10)  FEYWILD     Verdant Pyre      Dragon-Wraith            "the wellspring is blighting"
T III(11–16) SHADOWFELL  Hoard-Kingdom     Dracolich                "he has built a kingdom"
T IV (17–20) EMBERTHRONE the Source        Becoming God             "he is replacing death itself"
```

## 4. The four tiers in detail

Each tier below gives: the **plane & gateway**, the **spine of play**, **2–3 key
set-pieces**, the **tier boss beat**, and the **growth trigger** (what advances the
villain, the items, and the clocks).

### TIER I — THE MARROWMOOR (Material Plane), levels 1–4

**Gateway:** none — this is home. A fog-bound borderland of moors, barrow-mounds, and a
single struggling town, **Lantern's Rest**, whose dead have begun walking back to their
own doorsteps.

**Spine:** Start small and human. The party are locals or newcomers hired to deal with
"grave-trouble." They uncover that the restlessness radiates from an old barrow-complex,
the **Marrowmoor Vault**, where a robed figure — **the Herald** (Halthurax's mortal
agent, a death-priest carrying a shard of his will) — is performing the anchoring rite
that lets the Dimming reach this region.

**Key set-pieces**
1. **The doorstep dead** (lvl 1). A returned villager won't stop coming home. First
   undead fight; first chance to *return vs. scatter* a mote. Introduces the moral micro-choice.
2. **The Vault descent** (lvl 2–3). A classic barrow dungeon — traps, ghoul packs, a
   guardian **wight** — culminating in the Herald's ritual chamber.
3. **The Bonding** (lvl 1, session-zero payoff). At a barrow-altar each PC's chosen item
   drinks a mote of their own fire and *wakes* — the legacy items come online here.

**Tier boss beat:** The Herald, and behind/above the Vault, the party glimpses
**Halthurax himself — alive, vast, golden** — for one terrifying, un-fightable moment.
He speaks to them kindly. He is not the cackling-villain type; he warns them to stop, for
their own sake, and leaves. (He is CR ~17 here; this is a *scene*, not a fight.)

**Growth trigger:** Destroying the Herald frees the local hoard (good!) but completes the
*anchor* (bad — Halthurax no longer needs the Material Plane and withdraws fully into the
planes, beginning his molt to Wraith). **First legacy-item milestone fires at the start
of Tier II.** Conflict clocks all set their opening positions here.

**The revenant ally enters (Conflict #3):** In the Vault the party can free **Sera Vant**,
a knight whose mote was half-claimed — a **revenant** who should be hostile but instead
latches onto the party as her only tether to staying *herself.* She joins. The thread back
to Halthurax is already in her chest.

---

### TIER II — THE VERDANT PYRE (Feywild), levels 5–10

**Gateway:** A fey crossing in the deep Marrowmoor woods, opened by following Sera's pull
or an Emberwarden guide. The Feywild here is where soul-fire blooms *visibly* — a realm of
living light, the **Verdant Pyre** — and it is going gray at the edges. Halthurax is
blighting the wellspring of vitality to feed his ascent.

**Spine:** This is the tier where the **Emberwardens (Conflict #1)** become central. Their
fractured fey-court chapter, the **Court of First Light**, is split between the
**Keepers** (return the dead — the old way) and the **Tallymasters** (hoard against the
Dimming — Halthurax's logic, dressed in mercy). The party's deeds across the tier tip the
court, and the court's allegiance determines who fights *with* them at the finale.

**Key set-pieces**
1. **The graying glades** (lvl 5–6). Fey of light and growth twisted into wraith-things;
   the party learns blight spreads from a wound in the world.
2. **The Tallymasters' bargain** (lvl 7–8, mostly social). A powerful archfey offers the
   party a shortcut — accept hoarded fey-light to power up faster. Taking it is real power
   and real corruption of Conflict #1. Refusing is harder and slower. *No combat required.*
3. **The Dragon-Wraith's hunt** (lvl 9). Halthurax's second form stalks the party through
   the dimming Feywild — a chase/skirmish set-piece where he can't yet be killed, only driven off.

**Tier boss beat:** At the wound in the world — the **Pyre's Heart** — the party confronts
the **Dragon-Wraith** (CR ~13). Defeating it heals the Feywild (huge, visible good) and
frees a second swathe of hoard. But the freed motes pour *toward the Source*, and Halthurax
rides them down into the **Shadowfell**, completing his lichdom. Second molt.

**Growth trigger:** **Second legacy-item milestone fires.** Court of First Light's final
lean (Keeper vs. Tallymaster) is locked here — record it. Sera's thread tightens: she
starts losing memories to Halthurax (Conflict #3 escalates).

---

### TIER III — THE HOARD-KINGDOM (Shadowfell), levels 11–16

**Gateway:** Through a soul-fall in the Shadowfell — you follow the freed motes down. The
Shadowfell here is no longer a wasteland; Halthurax has **built a kingdom** out of hoarded
souls: the gray city of **Ossuara**, where the comfortable dead serve a king who promises
they'll never have to return.

**Spine:** The horror tier, and the most morally loaded. Ossuara is not a torment-pit — it
is *pleasant*, which is the point. Its citizens are happy hoarded souls who do not want to
be "freed." The party must decide what they're actually here to do: liberate people who'd
rather stay dead? This is where Halthurax's thesis is most seductive, and where a corrupted
Emberwarden party may simply... agree.

**Key set-pieces**
1. **The grateful dead** (lvl 11–12). Social/exploration through Ossuara; the party meets
   souls they personally "returned" earlier who are *angry* at them for it.
2. **The death-court** (lvl 13–14). Politics among the dracolich's lieutenants —
   **bone-knight generals, an undead red dragon vassal**, a chancellor who is Sera's
   original liege. Conflict #3 can resolve here: Sera confronts the lord who damned her.
3. **The siege of the Pale Hoard** (lvl 15). A full undead-army set-piece assaulting the
   vault where Halthurax keeps the densest part of his phylactery.

**Tier boss beat:** **Halthurax the Dracolich** (CR ~21) in his throne-vault. This is the
first time he can be *truly* hurt — and the first time a PC death is *expected* (§7). Beating
him here shatters the kingdom and frees the great mass of the hoard. But the freed motes,
nowhere to return (the Source is failing from the Dimming), follow Halthurax up to the one
place still warm: **the Source itself.** Final molt: he goes to *become* it.

**Growth trigger:** **Third legacy-item milestone fires.** The Ember clock (Conflict #2)
hits its crisis threshold — resurrection now has full teeth (§7). Whether the freed souls
have anywhere to go depends on how full the Source still is, which depends on how the party
played Conflict #2 the whole way down.

---

### TIER IV — THE EMBERTHRONE (the Source), levels 17–20

**Gateway:** Ascend the soul-stream itself, against the current, to the **Emberthrone** —
a half-built plane Halthurax is raising *around* the dying Source so that he becomes the
gate every soul must pass through. He is no longer hiding his pity; he believes he is one
ritual away from ending death forever, and he means to take the party with him into eternity.

**Spine:** Everything pays off. The Emberwardens you redeemed or corrupted arrive as your
army or your enemies (Conflict #1). The Ember clock decides whether the Source can be
*refilled* or only *defended* (Conflict #2). Sera's fate — defector, martyr, or freed
woman — lands here (Conflict #3). The legacy items hit their **capstone** form.

**Key set-pieces**
1. **The Choir of the Hoarded** (lvl 17–18). The assembled stolen souls, sung into a single
   chorused mind, defend the Throne; the party must *unmake the hoard* one last time —
   which means convincing or releasing souls who've been dead for a thousand years.
2. **The Two Endings of Death** (lvl 19, the campaign's thesis scene). Halthurax offers a
   final, genuine bargain: help him *succeed* and no one ever dies again — including the
   PCs' own dead. The party can *take it.* The campaign supports both answers (§6).
3. **The Becoming God** (lvl 20). Final battle (CR ~26 across phases) at the lip of the
   Source. Halthurax fights with the whole hoard as hit points; the party wins by *emptying*
   him, returning the embers, even at the cost of their own.

**Capstone & ending:** §6.

## 5. The three non-combat conflicts (the slow burn)

These run all 20 levels and resolve through **choices, relationships, and resource
management — never a required fight.** Each is a tracked clock or meter the GM nudges at
tier breaks.

### Conflict #1 — Redeem or Corrupt: the Emberwardens
**Meter:** a scale from **Keepers (+)** ↔ **Tallymasters (−)**, starting at 0.
The Emberwardens are Halthurax's *own old order*, now split between returning the dead and
hoarding against the Dimming. The party tips them by deeds: every time they **return** a
mote instead of scattering it, honor a Keeper, refuse a hoarding shortcut → +. Every
shortcut taken, every "merciful" hoard, every Tallymaster favor → −.
**Payoff:** at Tier IV the Wardens arrive as allies (Keeper) or as a second enemy army that
agrees with Halthurax (Tallymaster). Mid-campaign, the meter gates intel, sanctuary, and
resurrection access. *Redeeming them repudiates the villain's thesis; corrupting them proves
it.*

### Conflict #2 — The Dwindling Resource: the Ember itself
**Meter:** the **Ember Clock**, a pool that only ever decreases — the world's remaining
soul-fire. Starts at 12 segments. Drops 1 at each tier break automatically (the Dimming),
plus 1 each time the party **scatters** a mote for speed instead of returning it, minus
nothing — you can only *slow* it, never refill it until the very end.
**Effects by threshold:**
- 12–9: the world is merely "off." Resurrection magic works normally.
- 8–5: the Dimming is visible. Raise/Revivify costs **1 extra Ember segment** (a real,
  shared, dwindling resource) on top of components.
- 4–2 (locked in by Tier III): resurrection requires *spending another living being's
  mote*, or returns the PC **Touched** (§7).
- 1–0: no natural resurrection remains; the only way back is at the Source in Tier IV.
**This is the spine of player-death stakes (§7).**

### Conflict #3 — The Fraying Loyalty: Sera Vant, the revenant ally
**Meter:** the **Tether** — how much of Sera is *hers* vs. Halthurax's, from 10 (whole) to
0 (his). Starts at 7 (she's already half-claimed when you meet her).
She loses a point at each tier break (his ascent pulls her) and can *gain* points only
through the party's investment: keeping promises to her, helping her confront her past
(the chancellor in Tier III), choosing the merciful-but-costly options she cares about.
**Resolutions (decided by the Tether and the party's choices, no boss fight required):**
- **Tether 7+ at finale:** Sera is freed — she returns her own mote willingly and dies at
  peace, or survives reincarnated, the campaign's gentlest grace note.
- **Tether 3–6:** Sera *martyrs* herself — spends her remaining self to open the path to the
  Source, the party's costliest ally.
- **Tether 0–2:** Sera **defects** — Halthurax claims her, and she stands among his lieutenants
  at the Emberthrone. The party's failure with one person, made flesh.

## 6. Endings (player death is expected; so is moral weight)

The thesis scene (Tier IV, §4) is real: the party can **side with Halthurax**, and the
campaign supports it. Track which of three endings the party earns:

- **The Old Order Restored (refuse him, refill the Source).** Achievable only with a healthy
  Ember Clock and redeemed Wardens. The party returns the hoard, the Source rekindles, death
  resumes its rightful, sad, necessary work. Their own dead stay dead — and that is the price
  of a living world. The hardest and truest heroic-dark-fantasy ending.
- **The Pyrrhic Throne (defeat him with a gutted Ember Clock).** They win, but the Source is
  too drained to refill; the world survives diminished, fewer souls, a long winter of
  scarcity. Victory that costs the future.
- **The Mercy of Never (join him).** No one ever dies again — including everyone the party
  loved. And the world slowly stops being born, a paradise with no children, ruled by a
  grieving god and his hero-lieutenants. The seductive ending, played straight, not punished
  with a cheap "gotcha."

**Capstone for survivors:** each surviving PC's legacy item reaches its final form (Appendix
B). PCs who *died and were returned at the Source* in Tier IV come back **whole** — the one
place the Dimming can be undone — closing the death arc on a note of grace.

## 7. Death with teeth (the player-death subsystem)

Player death is expected and *means something* because it's wired to Conflict #2.

- **On a PC's death**, their mote tries to return to the Source — but Halthurax's drain
  contests it. Resurrection is governed by the **Ember Clock** thresholds in §5.
- **The Touched table (returns at Clock 4–2):** a PC raised while the Ember is low comes
  back changed — Shadowfell-marked. Roll or choose: a cosmetic mark (ash-gray veins, a cold
  shadow), a new **flaw** (a hunger, a fear of warmth, a name they answer to that isn't
  theirs), and a small **edge** (resistance to necrotic; advantage on death saves; a once-per-
  long-rest minor necrotic strike). Touched is a story state, not a penalty spiral.
- **Permanent loss is on the table** at Clock 1–0 until Tier IV — and that's by design. A PC
  who dies in Tier III may simply be *gone* until the Source is reached, where a single great
  act of return can restore the fallen. This makes the climb to the Emberthrone personal.
- **Return vs. Scatter** (the recurring undead micro-choice) is how players *feel* their hand
  on the Clock all campaign long, long before anyone dies.

## 8. Session-zero kit

- **Pitch in one line:** "A fallen guardian is hoarding the world's souls to end death
  forever — and he might be right. Stop him before the world runs out of life to be born."
- **Bonded items:** each player picks an object meaningful to their PC (a weapon, a holy
  symbol, an heirloom, an instrument). It becomes their ember-forged legacy item at the
  Tier I barrow-altar. Build it together using `Appendix-B-Legacy-Items.md`.
- **Tone dial:** heroic dark fantasy. Real dread, real loss — but the party are heroes, color
  and warmth survive between the horror, and mercy is always *available*, just costly.
- **The three clocks** (Wardens meter, Ember Clock, Sera's Tether): show players the Ember
  Clock openly (they should feel it drain); keep the Wardens meter and the Tether semi-hidden,
  revealed through fiction.
- **Safety:** undeath, grief, and "is death good?" are core themes. Line up tools (X-card,
  lines & veils) in session zero.
- **Expectation-setting:** tell players up front — *death is part of this story, and coming
  back is never free.* That's the game.

## 9. Running notes & dragons-as-a-plus

- **Dragons:** Halthurax is the spine, but seed living dragons as *foils* — a frightened young
  gold who remembers what Halthurax was (Tier I–II ally), the **undead red dragon vassal** in
  Ossuara (Tier III), and the choice in Tier IV of whether to redeem or destroy his
  dragon-lieutenants. The campaign is, underneath, an argument *among dragons* about death.
- **Pacing the molts:** never let a tier-boss "kill" feel like a cheat. Always give the party a
  real, visible *win for the world* (a region healed, a kingdom freed) in the same beat that
  reveals the villain molted. Hope and dread in the same scene — that's the register.
- **CR scaling:** Halthurax's forms (Appendix A) are tuned to be beatable at each tier's
  *top*, not its bottom. The early Material-Plane sighting is deliberately un-fightable — sell
  it as awe, not a TPK.
